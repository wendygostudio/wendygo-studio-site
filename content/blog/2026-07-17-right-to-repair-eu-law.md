Something broke. The manufacturer says it's out of warranty and you should just buy a new one. EU law now says that's not always their call to make.

## What Is the EU Right to Repair?

The Right to Repair refers to a set of EU regulations that require manufacturers and sellers to support repair over replacement. The core idea: extending the lifespan of products benefits consumers, the environment, and competition in repair markets.

The **EU Right to Repair Directive (Directive 2024/1799)**, adopted in April 2024, is the most significant piece of this framework. It creates enforceable consumer rights around repair — not voluntary manufacturer promises.

This is separate from the standard [EU 2-year warranty](/blog/eu-warranty-rights-explained/), which covers defects during the warranty period. The Right to Repair goes further: it imposes obligations *after* the warranty expires too.

## What Changed with the 2024 Directive?

Before 2024, EU consumer rights around repair were fragmented. Manufacturers had no legal obligation to maintain repair infrastructure beyond the warranty period. The 2024 Directive introduced concrete obligations:

**During the 2-year warranty period:**
- Sellers must offer repair as an alternative to replacement when repair is no more expensive.

**After warranty expiry:**
- Producers of covered products must provide repair services for a fixed number of years (5–10 years depending on product category).
- Spare parts and tools must be available to independent repair shops at fair, non-discriminatory prices.
- Manufacturers cannot use software locks or contractual clauses to block independent repairers.
- Using refurbished or 3D-printed parts is explicitly allowed, provided safety standards are met.

## Which Products Are Covered?

The Directive applies to product categories regulated under EU ecodesign rules:

- Smartphones and tablets
- Laptops and computers
- Televisions
- Washing machines and washer-dryers
- Dishwashers
- Refrigerators and freezers
- Vacuum cleaners (including robot vacuums)
- Power tools and certain agricultural machinery
- Welding equipment
- Servers and storage products

Not every product is on the list. If yours isn't covered, you may still have rights under the standard 2-year legal warranty if the defect appeared within that period.

## How to Exercise Your Right to Repair

If your product breaks and it falls within the warranty period:

1. **Contact the seller in writing** — email creates a traceable paper trail.
2. **State that you want repair**, and cite EU Directive 2011/83/EU and 2024/1799 as your legal basis.
3. **If the seller pushes replacement**, ask them in writing to explain why repair is not being offered.
4. **If repair is refused or takes unreasonably long**, escalate — you have grounds to file a formal complaint.

If you're outside the warranty period but the manufacturer is obligated under the 2024 Directive, contact them directly. Request repair service citing Directive 2024/1799, state your product category, and ask for a written timeline and cost estimate.

## What If the Seller or Manufacturer Refuses?

Refusals happen. Here's how to respond:

1. **Ask for the refusal in writing.** A seller who refuses repair without citing a valid legal exception is creating a paper trail that works against them.
2. **Contact your national consumer authority.** Every EU country has an enforcement body — they investigate violations and can compel compliance.
3. **File a formal complaint.** The [step-by-step complaint process](/blog/how-to-file-consumer-complaint-eu/) covers how to escalate through national and EU channels.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does the EU right to repair mean repairs are free?**
No. You generally pay for out-of-warranty repairs. What the law guarantees is *access* to repair — spare parts, authorized service, and no artificial blocks on independent repairers.

**Can I choose any repair shop I want?**
Yes. Manufacturers cannot restrict you to their own service centers. Independent repairers must have access to spare parts and tools at fair prices.

**Does this apply to products bought outside the EU?**
It applies if you bought from an EU-based seller and the product is in a covered category. If you bought from a non-EU seller shipping internationally, your rights may differ.

**What if the manufacturer says repair is "economically unviable"?**
This is a common tactic but has legal limits. The Directive restricts the use of "unviability" as a blanket excuse. If you're given this response, request it in writing and escalate to your national consumer authority.

**Is the Right to Repair Directive already in force?**
The Directive entered into force in July 2024. EU member states have until mid-2026 to fully transpose it into national law. Coverage and enforcement vary by country during the transition — check your national consumer authority's guidance.
