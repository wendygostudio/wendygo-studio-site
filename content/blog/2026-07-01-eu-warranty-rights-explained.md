# EU 2-Year Warranty Rights Explained: What You're Entitled To and How to Claim It

You bought a laptop that stopped working after 14 months. The seller tells you the warranty is only 12 months and there's nothing they can do. You accept it because you don't know the law.

That's exactly what retailers count on.

EU law gives you a **minimum 2-year legal guarantee** on any product you buy from an EU seller. It doesn't matter what the box says, what the seller's "policy" is, or whether the brand is based in another country. The law supersedes all of that.

Here's what you're actually entitled to — and how to claim it.

## What the EU Legal Guarantee Says

The EU Directive 2019/771, which came into force in January 2022, standardizes consumer rights across all EU member states. The key points:

- **2-year minimum guarantee** from the date of delivery, for all physical goods
- Applies to defects that **existed at the time of delivery**, even if they appear later
- Covers goods bought **in-store, online, or via marketplace sellers** based in the EU
- The seller — not the manufacturer — is responsible for honoring this guarantee
- The guarantee is **free of charge** to the consumer

Some EU countries extend this further. France, for example, has a 2-year burden-of-proof period; Portugal offers 3 years. But 2 years is the EU-wide floor.

## What the Guarantee Covers — And What It Doesn't

**Covered:**
- Manufacturing defects (hardware that fails, components that break under normal use)
- Goods that don't match the description, sample, or model you saw before buying
- Features that were advertised but don't work
- Products that lack normal quality or durability for their category

**Not covered:**
- Damage you caused (drops, liquid, physical abuse)
- Normal wear and tear (battery degradation, surface scratches)
- Problems you were told about before purchase
- Consumables that wear out as intended (printer ink, razor blades)

If the defect appears in the **first year**, EU law presumes it existed at delivery — the seller must prove otherwise. This presumption now extends to 2 years in several member states following the 2022 directive update.

## How to File a Warranty Claim: 4 Steps

**Step 1: Document everything**
Take photos or video of the defect. Note the date it appeared and the exact symptom. Keep your purchase receipt, order confirmation, or bank statement.

**Step 2: Contact the seller in writing**
Email or use the seller's contact form. State clearly: the product has a defect, when it appeared, and that you're invoking your legal guarantee rights under EU Directive 2019/771. Written requests create a paper trail.

**Step 3: Request your remedy**
You're entitled to ask for:
- **Repair** (first option if the seller can do it at no cost and within a reasonable time)
- **Replacement** (if repair is impossible or disproportionately expensive)
- **Price reduction** or **full refund** (if repair/replacement fails, takes too long, or causes significant inconvenience)

**Step 4: Escalate if the seller refuses**
If the seller ignores you or refuses without valid reason, you can:
- File a complaint with your national consumer protection authority
- Use the EU Online Dispute Resolution (ODR) platform at ec.europa.eu/consumers/odr
- Contact a consumer association in your country for free legal support

## The Right to Repair (EU Directive 2024/1799)

Since 2024, EU consumers also have a **right to repair** for certain product categories, including washing machines, dishwashers, refrigerators, televisions, smartphones, tablets, and laptops.

This means manufacturers must:
- Offer repair services at a reasonable price
- Provide spare parts and tools to independent repair shops
- Not use software locks that prevent third-party repairs

The right to repair extends beyond the 2-year guarantee window. Even after your legal guarantee expires, you're entitled to have certain products repaired by the manufacturer.

## What to Do If the Seller Refuses

A seller who refuses your valid warranty claim is breaking EU law. Your options:

1. **Send a formal complaint letter** — clearly state the legal basis (EU Directive 2019/771, your country's consumer law)
2. **Contact your national consumer authority** — they can intervene and fine non-compliant sellers
3. **File an ODR dispute** — for online purchases, the EU's dispute resolution platform handles cross-border cases
4. **Chargeback** — if you paid by credit card and the seller is uncooperative, your bank may reverse the charge under dispute protection rules

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does the 2-year warranty apply to products bought online from EU sellers?**
Yes. The EU legal guarantee applies to all purchases from sellers established in the EU, whether in-store or online. For marketplace purchases, the liability falls on the individual seller, not the platform.

**What if the seller says the warranty is only 1 year?**
They're wrong. A commercial warranty shorter than 2 years doesn't override your legal guarantee rights. The legal guarantee is a minimum set by law and cannot be waived by contract.

**Can I go directly to the manufacturer instead of the seller?**
Your legal guarantee runs against the seller, not the manufacturer. If the manufacturer offered a separate commercial warranty, you can pursue that independently — but your EU rights are against whoever sold you the product.

**What's the difference between the legal guarantee and a commercial warranty?**
The legal guarantee is mandatory EU law — you have it whether or not the seller mentions it. A commercial warranty is a voluntary additional promise from the seller or manufacturer, which may have different terms and coverage. You can use both, but you can never lose the legal guarantee.

**Does the warranty period restart after a repair?**
It depends on the country. In some member states, a repair or replacement resets the 2-year clock. In others, it extends it by a fixed period. Check your national consumer law for the specific rule.
