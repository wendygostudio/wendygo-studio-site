The EU's ODR Platform Is Gone: What to Use Instead

The EU's Online Dispute Resolution platform stopped taking new cases in March 2025 and closed for good on 20 July 2025. If you still have ec.europa.eu/consumers/odr bookmarked, it now leads nowhere useful.

July 24, 2026

Editorial legal review: 2026-07-24 · European Union; national rules may add or vary protections

General information; national rules and case circumstances may vary. This is not legal advice.

Official sources: 1 · 2 · 3 · 4

Say you bought something from a seller in another EU country, it arrived broken, and the seller is ignoring you. A year ago the advice was simple: file it on the EU's Online Dispute Resolution platform and let it route the case for you. That platform is gone now, and a surprising number of guides — ours included, until an earlier cleanup pass — still pointed at it.

What actually closed The European Online Dispute Resolution (ODR) platform stopped accepting new complaints in March 2025 and shut down completely on 20 July 2025, under Regulation (EU) 2024/3228. All user data on the platform was deleted after closure.

Why the European Commission closed it

The platform's own usage numbers were the official reason: about 200 disputes a year across the entire EU, and only roughly 2% of complaints got a response from the trader through the system. For a tool meant to serve nearly 450 million consumers, that's a rounding error. The Commission's site-relocation notice frames the closure as a cost and efficiency decision — not a change in what rights you have.

What replaced it

There's no single successor site that automatically routes your complaint the way the old platform did. Instead, you have two separate paths depending on what kind of dispute it is:

Two different problems, two different doors

  • A cross-border purchase dispute (different EU country, Iceland or Norway) — contact the European Consumer Centre (ECC-Net) in your own country first, or go straight to the [official list of national dispute resolution bodies](https://consumer-redress.ec.europa.eu/dispute-resolution-bodies).
  • An unfair account or content decision by an online platform — this was never really what the EU ODR platform, which has closed, handled well, and it's explicitly separate: use one of the out-of-court dispute settlement bodies certified under Article 21 of the Digital Services Act.

What to actually do now

Workflow Figure out which door applies, then use it directly

If the seller is in your own country, skip ECC-Net entirely and go straight to your national ADR body or small claims procedure. If the seller is abroad, ECC-Net can explain your options in your language and may help negotiate directly with the trader before any formal ADR filing. Either way, keep your order confirmation, correspondence and any delivery or defect evidence together before you contact anyone — that's what [ClaimForge](/claimforge/) organizes automatically as you build a claim.

Nothing about the underlying law changed. The 2-year legal guarantee still applies, you can still file a formal complaint with the seller directly, and — if a lawyer isn't worth it for the amount in dispute — the free and low-cost alternatives we've covered before are still there. The only thing that disappeared is the single shared front door.

Before you search for "EU ODR" again

If a guide, a seller's terms page, or an old bookmark still points you at ec.europa.eu/consumers/odr, that's a sign the content hasn't been updated since mid-2025. It's not a scam or a broken link on your end — the European Commission retired the whole platform on purpose, and the redirect confirms it.

Frequently asked questions

Why did the EU shut down the ODR platform?

The European Commission cited low uptake: roughly 200 cases a year across the whole EU, and only about 2% of complaints led to a trader response through the platform. It was discontinued under Regulation (EU) 2024/3228 for efficiency and cost reasons, not because consumer rights changed.

What replaced the ODR platform for cross-border disputes?

There's no single automatic router anymore. For a dispute with a trader in another EU country, Iceland or Norway, contact the European Consumer Centre (ECC-Net) in your own country, or go directly to the relevant national ADR body from the European Commission's own list.

Do old links to the ODR platform still work?

No. ec.europa.eu/consumers/odr now redirects to a site-relocation notice on the European Commission's consumer-redress.ec.europa.eu domain confirming the closure, with pointers to ECC-Net and the national ADR body list. Any guide, template or terms-and-conditions page still citing the old URL is out of date.

Does this affect my actual consumer rights, or just this one tool?

Just the tool. The 2-year legal guarantee, the 14-day right of withdrawal, and your right to use ADR bodies or small claims court are unchanged. Only the single EU-run website that used to route complaints between countries is gone.
