If you've bookmarked `ec.europa.eu/consumers/odr` for a cross-border EU consumer dispute, it's time to update that bookmark. The European Commission shut the whole platform down.

## What actually happened

The EU's Online Dispute Resolution (ODR) platform stopped accepting new complaints in March 2025 and closed completely on 20 July 2025, under Regulation (EU) 2024/3228. All user data on the platform was wiped after closure.

The Commission's own stated reason is blunt: about 200 disputes a year across the entire EU, and only roughly 2% of complaints got a trader response through the system. For a tool meant to serve ~450 million consumers, that's basically nobody using it.

## The part that trips people up

There's no single successor site. The old platform did two things at once — it was a portal AND an automatic router to the right national ADR body in the seller's country. Both of those are gone as a single combined tool. What's left:

- **Cross-border purchase dispute** (different EU country, Iceland, Norway) → contact your national European Consumer Centre (ECC-Net), or go straight to the [official list of national ADR bodies](https://consumer-redress.ec.europa.eu/dispute-resolution-bodies).
- **Unfair account/content decision on a platform** → this was never really an EU-ODR case anyway. Use an out-of-court dispute settlement body certified under DSA Article 21.

Neither route auto-routes you the way the old platform did. You pick the door yourself.

## What didn't change

Your actual rights are untouched — the 2-year legal guarantee, the 14-day right of withdrawal, ADR access, small claims court. Only the single shared front door disappeared.

## Why this is still worth a post in July 2026

A year after the shutdown, a surprising number of guides, seller T&Cs pages, and old Stack Overflow / forum answers still cite the dead URL. We found the same stale reference in our own site's content until a recent audit pass — turns out a script we'd written specifically to retire ODR mentions (yes, we saw this coming back in 2025) hadn't caught every instance. If you maintain any consumer-facing docs that mention EU dispute resolution, it's worth a search-and-check.
